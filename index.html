Project Sports Covid-19

This website has been built as part of my Data Science module at the Univeristy of Bristol. Over a period of ten weeks I have completed weekly tasks using different data science techniques and have displayed them on my website. By the end of the ten weeks I aim to tell an interesting data story using a variety of data visualisations and commentary. This story can be found on my project page. This page contains the majority of my weekly tasks, however, I have decided to separate the tasks related to Covid-19 and Sports as they are easily categorised

FRED Charts - Week 6: Using for loops to download data

The data for the following charts was taken from FRED using a mini-dashboard that I have built in Google collaboratory. The dashboard uses a for loop to download multiple files at once from different API URLs.

This chart takes a look at the UK Unemployment rate over time. It is interesting to note that there was near to zero unemployment around the time of both World War 1 and World War 2.

This chart takes a look at the average weekly hours worked in the UK over time. There is a clear decrease in the amount of hours worked in the 19th century which could be attributed to the innovations of the industrial revolution leading to higher productivity.

This chart takes a look at UK total factor productivity, which is defined as the residual growth which happens beyond the growth of an economy's factors of production (e.g. Labour and Capital).

This chart shows the total value of all listed shares in the UK stock market as a percentage of GDP over time.

The Economics Observatory's Festival of economics - Week 8: A critical commentary

During the Economics Observatory's conference, one of the panelists claimed that one effect of the pandemic was a widening in the educational attainment gap between males and Females. The graph above supports this argument by showing that the percentage of A*'s achieved has increased for girls much more significantly than for boys.

Week 9: Advanced data analytics

This chart displays a regression of Nike share prices on Adidas Share prices from 2016 to 2021. As you can see the R-squared value is 0.77 which would suggest this regression is relatively strong, with most of the variation in Nike share prices explaining the variation in Adidas share prices. The data was taken from the Yahoo Finance python library using Google Collaboratory.
